snickerdoodle

ux research & product design

aug 2016 - dec 2016



Does the public education school system make children less creative?
why creativity?
I tutored 2nd and 3rd graders in Oakland last fall. I noticed my students were constantly being taught that questions had one correct answer: in other words, convergent thinking.
Convergent thinking is necessary and valuable. However, being trained in only one type of problem-solving may cause students to potentially lose the ability to think creatively and divergently.
So my team and I asked, how might we help elementary school students remain creative?
ux research: ethnographic interviews
Using a user-centered design process, our product design was heavily informed by user research.
We interviewed students, elementary school teachers, parents, school administration, researchers, and education professors.
We found that
Because of administrative work, teachers lack the time to incorporate creative curriculum into their lecture.
and
high-energy elementary school students need structured chaos to practice creativity

Our interviews and observations led us to the insights revealing the daily challenges that both students and teachers face. We then took the experiences and narratives of our users, laid them out, framed problems, and looked for insights.

students
Students are less likely to be creative when they feel that their voices are not valued in the classroom.
teachers
Teachers need the time they lost doing administrative work to prepare effective curriculum.
So we asked the question:

How might we help make both students and teachers feel valued?

the challenge
Though we got to the root issue we wanted to address, our solution could still be anything. As we worked to transition from needs finding to potential solutions, we dug deeper into understanding our users' problems.
My team and I created personas, journey maps, and organized our thoughts into 2x2 matrices as shown below.

In creating personas, we closely examined a day in the life of students and teachers, seeing if there was any specific point in the day our solution could target.

In the journey map, we decided to target what might be slumps in the school day for both students and teachers, and energize a classroom with an activity that allowed them to flex their creativity.

design principals
After talking to our users and getting a better picture of practical creativity, we developed a set of goals we wanted our solution to accomplish and synthesized them into a set of design principals.
the what
1. foster divergent thinking
2. make students' knowledge from outside a classroom valuable
3. adapt to specific classroom needs
4. level the playing field between teachers and students
5. develop empathy in students
6. be easily implemented into the school day


the how
by facilitating open ended discussion
by requiring students to draw from informal and formally-learned knowledge
by giving multiple ways to interact with the activity
by building off previous answers that are not soley provided by teachers
by imagining a day in someone else's life
by giving easy control of how long the game lasts
ideation
From these design principals, we came up with 15+ activities we hypothesized would hit all of our design principals and landed on something that could most seamlessly and effortlessly be integrated with the teacher’s existing curriculum, so we proceeded with Snickerdoodle.
the solution
You’ve got three decks.
The following activity is then shaped around bringing the person to life.
An example of playing the question and answer game is the following:
  • When does this person sleep?
  • What do they eat for lunch?
  • Who is this person’s best friend?
  • How does this person get to work?
  • How would this person respond in the following scenarios? And give students a scenario. For example: waiting at the coffee shop, someone accidentally spills coffee on this person. How does this person respond?
  • What are some adjectives that might describe this person? Why?
  • What does this person do in his/her free time?
  • What is the most important thing to this person?
  • What’s this person’s fav color? Why?
  • If you could ask this person anything, what would it be?
  • What makes this person happy?
  • What is this person afraid of?
user testing
We took the game to the classroom!
During our user testing, we uncovered more problems and assumptions that we had about the way the game would work and the way classroom interactions work.
Assumptions uncovered:
  • 2nd - 3rd grade students are generally quiet individuals who don’t have opportunities to speak up in class.
  • Students are good listeners. They will retain what others say and build off of existing conversation.
  • Students will avoid fixating on things already said and will come up with original ideas each time a new student speaks.
  • Students will let other students speak and wait their turn before adding to the discussion.
  • It is the student’s natural inclination to want to obey authority- and they need to learn how to challenge authority instead.
  • The students will focus on the task at hand in group activities.
In my 2nd – 3rd grade classroom, order, authority, and focus are a few of the most important things that students learn. Students normally just want to verbalize whatever is on their mind and do whatever they want when given the chance. I personally did not speak up in class in elementary school. But I was not the end user. This class may have its own personality compared to other classes, but these characteristics made it difficult to play the game UNLESS the class was led by a strong teacher who was able to assert authority and moderate discussion.
Observations:
This was a very chaotic game. Students got way too excited by the game, and it made them want to get out of their seats and start enacting different characteristic traits.
Getting students excited but also focused is another issue. Though students say interesting things diverging off topic, productive creativity needs structure and focus.
next steps
Define the metrics: finding measurable and detailed ways to track student's progress that make creativity into a systematically trackable quality
In future iterations, I would consider including a way to reward active listening and building discussion off of previous comments. I could also test penalizing any repeated comments.
